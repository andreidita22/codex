This is a **real upgrade** over the first note. It explicitly captures the missing long-term concept: the broker as a **bounded semantic adjudicator over a typed schema registry**, not just a small deterministic verb matcher. The strongest improvement is that it now cleanly separates:

* **canonical operator meta-schema**
* **workflow/procedure schema instances**
* **alias/synonym tables**
* **runtime registry snapshot**
* **candidate narrowing + adjudication**
* **typed resolution / packet construction**

That is the right architecture, and it fixes the main risk in the first draft, namely that v1 could accidentally fossilize into a hard-coded mini resolver instead of the first implementation of a richer bounded-selection system. The revised note also does a very good job of baking future extensibility into day-1 types: `SchemaRegistrySnapshot`, `CandidateSet`, `SchemaAdjudicator`, `ResolutionConfidence`, `LexicalEvolutionEvent`, and the richer packet fields are exactly the kind of abstractions that let you keep slice 1 narrow while preserving the real trajectory. 

It also finally makes the **interactive schema-authoring mode** first-class in the right way. The key win there is that it does **not** treat schema creation as “paste more markdown,” but as a bounded workflow under a meta-schema, with guided field collection, validation, alias-vs-new-schema distinction, draft/proposed/approved lifecycle, and repo-native persistence. That is much closer to your actual vision of skill/schema creation as a structured co-authoring process with a bounded agent that understands the creation law itself. The clarification UX section is also much better than before: narrow, schema-aware, operational clarifications instead of vague “what did you mean?” prompts. And the multi-track section now clearly ties schema resolution to **track-scoped foregrounding**, which is one of the biggest architectural wins of the whole broker idea. 

My remaining comments are mostly refinement, not objection. First, I would make **registry source precedence and conflict resolution** more explicit: if builtin, project, user, skill, and governance schemas all define overlapping candidates, there should be a typed resolution law for who wins, or at least how conflicts are surfaced. Second, I would keep an eye on the packet growing too rich too early; the revised packet is much better than the first draft, but you still want the broker packet to stay mostly **refs + bindings + confidence + events**, not become a second giant semantic document. Third, the schema-authoring mode may eventually deserve its own adjacent crate once it grows beyond validation/state machine logic, but for now keeping it broker-adjacent is fine. Overall though, this second note is much closer to the architecture you actually want: same good v1 seam and overlay strategy, but now with the proper long-term ontology of **bounded semantic broker choosing among repo-native schemas** built in from day 1.
